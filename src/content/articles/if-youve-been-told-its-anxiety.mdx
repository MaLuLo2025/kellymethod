You have been here before.

A doctor's office, or maybe an urgent care, or maybe a cardiologist who gave you ten minutes. The intake form was long. The waiting room was cold. You explained, again, that your heart races when you stand. That you have to sit down in the shower. That you forget words mid-sentence and your mother is starting to worry. That you fainted at the grocery store last spring and the EMT who responded was the first person who took you seriously, and even he just told you to drink more water.

You explained, and then you watched the doctor type for a while, and then they looked up and said: "Have you considered that this might be anxiety?"

Maybe they were kind about it. Maybe they suggested therapy gently, like they were doing you a favor. Maybe they wrote you a prescription for an SSRI on the first visit, before any tests came back. Maybe they were less kind. Maybe they implied, without quite saying so, that you were a young woman who had read too much on the internet.

You went home. You took the medication or you didn't. You tried therapy or you'd already been in therapy for years. You waited to see if it would help — and it didn't, because the thing that was wrong with you was not, and had never been, anxiety.

If any of this sounds familiar, this article is for you.

## What the research actually says

You are not making this up, and you are not the only one. The pattern you have been living through is well-documented and it has a name. It is the standard delay in diagnosing Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome — POTS — and the typical patient experiences it for somewhere between four and six years before someone correctly identifies what is happening to them.

In that time, the typical patient sees seven different doctors. Most of them — well over half, in patient surveys conducted by Dysautonomia International and other patient advocacy organizations — receive a psychiatric diagnosis along the way. Most commonly anxiety. Sometimes depression. Sometimes a vague catch-all like "somatization" or "functional disorder." Almost none of these diagnoses are correct.

The condition you actually have is measurable. It can be confirmed at the bedside in ten minutes with a blood pressure cuff and a heart rate monitor. The diagnostic criteria have been published in major cardiology journals. Several million Americans have it.

A study of POTS patients published in the European Journal of Internal Medicine found that 75% had been told their symptoms were psychiatric before receiving a correct diagnosis. The number is staggering, and it is also unsurprising once you understand the structural reasons it keeps happening.

## Why the medical system keeps making this mistake

It is worth being precise about why this happens, because precision is the first step to working around it. The pattern is not the result of any individual doctor being incompetent or cruel — though some of them are. The pattern is the result of several specific failures that compound on top of each other, and you are not the first patient to encounter them.

**The symptoms genuinely overlap with anxiety.** A racing heart, shortness of breath, lightheadedness, trembling, a sense of doom. These are also the symptoms of a panic attack. A clinician who has been trained to recognize panic attacks but not POTS will, when faced with these symptoms, reach for what they know.

The difference, which a careful clinician would notice, is that POTS symptoms are triggered by *standing*, not by emotional stress. They are predictable in a way that panic is not. They get worse in the heat. They get worse after meals. They get better when you lie down. None of this is true of anxiety.

But asking about that distinction requires knowing it exists. Most physicians don't.

**The standard tests come back normal.** Your EKG looked fine. Your bloodwork was unremarkable. Your thyroid panel didn't flag. The echocardiogram, if you got one, showed nothing structural. None of this is surprising — POTS is a disorder of the autonomic nervous system, not the heart muscle. The standard cardiac workup is designed to catch other things.

The diagnostic test for POTS is not on the standard cardiac workup. It is the active stand test, sometimes called the 10-minute stand or the NASA lean test, and it costs nothing and takes ten minutes and most primary care offices have never heard of it.

In the absence of an obvious physical abnormality, and in the absence of a test that would have actually found one, your doctor defaulted to the diagnosis they had been trained to consider when nothing else fit. That diagnosis was anxiety.

**Most physicians have never been taught about POTS.** A 2019 survey of primary care physicians found that fewer than 30% could correctly identify POTS as a clinical entity. Most medical schools devote almost no curriculum time to dysautonomia. For a condition that affects an estimated one to three million Americans — more than multiple sclerosis, more than Parkinson's disease in younger patients — that gap in training is genuinely remarkable.

It is also why you have probably had the experience of explaining your symptoms to a series of doctors who looked at you with mild puzzlement, ran a few tests, and then offered you something other than an answer.

**You are probably a young woman.** Roughly 85% of POTS patients are female, and most experience symptom onset between adolescence and their mid-forties. There is a substantial body of research demonstrating that younger female patients with complex, non-specific symptoms are more likely to be dismissed, more likely to be told their symptoms are psychological, and more likely to wait longer for a correct diagnosis than male patients with comparable presentations.

This is not a polemic. It is a documented pattern. It is reported in major medical journals. It is named in physician training programs that try to address it. It is the reason that a male patient walking into an ER with chest pain and a racing heart is more likely to receive a cardiac workup, and a female patient walking into the same ER with the same symptoms is more likely to receive an anxiolytic.

You are running into this pattern. You did not invent it. You did not deserve it. It is real, and it is part of why you have spent years trying to figure out what is wrong with you.

## What to ask for next

Knowing the pattern is half the work. The other half is what to do about it.

The single most useful sentence you can say in your next medical appointment is this:

> *My symptoms are triggered by standing. Can we do a ten-minute stand test to check my heart rate response?*

That sentence does several things at once. It names the trigger — standing, not stress — which immediately distinguishes what you're describing from anxiety. It proposes a specific, named test. It asks for ten minutes of the doctor's time, which is a small enough ask that most clinicians will agree to it. And it puts the burden on the doctor to either perform the test or justify declining.

If your doctor doesn't know what the active stand test is, you can offer this: *"It's the standard screening test for POTS — postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome. I'd like to rule it out or in."*

The test itself is simple. You lie down for five to ten minutes while your heart rate and blood pressure are measured at rest. Then you stand up — not leaning against a wall — and your heart rate and blood pressure are measured again at one minute, three minutes, five minutes, and ten minutes.

If your heart rate sustains an increase of 30 beats per minute or more (40 beats per minute if you are between 12 and 19) within those ten minutes, and your blood pressure does not significantly drop, that result is consistent with POTS. It is not by itself a diagnosis — that requires confirmation from a clinician familiar with the condition — but it is the entry point to a real evaluation.

If your doctor refuses to perform the test, ask them to document the refusal in your chart. Then find a different doctor.

## What to push back on

There is a recognizable script that gets used to dismiss POTS symptoms, and it helps to have responses ready before you walk into the appointment.

If a doctor tells you, *"This sounds like anxiety,"* the response is: *"I understand anxiety is common, but my symptoms are triggered by standing, not emotional stress. I'd like to rule out POTS before treating this as anxiety."*

If a doctor tells you, *"Your tests came back normal,"* the response is: *"The standard cardiac workup is usually normal in POTS. POTS is diagnosed by a stand test, not by EKG."*

If a doctor tells you, *"Let's try an SSRI and see if that helps,"* the response is: *"I'm willing to consider that, but I'd like to rule out POTS first. Can we do the stand test today?"*

If a doctor tells you, *"POTS is very rare,"* the response is: *"Recent estimates put it at one to three million Americans. It's more common than multiple sclerosis."*

These responses are specific, calm, and grounded in clinical fact. They are not arguments. They are requests for a specific evaluation, framed in language that respects the doctor's expertise while declining to accept a diagnosis that doesn't fit.

The dynamic in these conversations matters. A patient who says *"I think you're wrong, I have POTS"* puts the doctor in a defensive posture. A patient who says *"I'd like to rule out POTS — can we do a ten-minute stand test?"* invites the doctor into a clinical conversation. The second framing is more likely to get the test ordered.

## What to do if you still aren't being heard

Some patients do all of this and still get dismissed. It happens. When it does, the path forward is procedural, not confrontational.

The single highest-leverage move is to find a different clinician. A doctor who has dismissed your symptoms once will almost always continue to dismiss them. You are not obligated to keep working with someone who does not take your condition seriously, and there is no virtue in patience with a doctor who is not going to help you. Your time is better spent finding someone who will.

Cardiologists, electrophysiologists, and autonomic neurologists are the specialists most likely to evaluate POTS competently. Wait times can be long — six months to a year at major academic centers — but you can often get the diagnostic stand test done by a primary care doctor or local cardiologist in the meantime. Once you have the documented heart rate response on paper, the door opens to other clinicians taking you seriously.

Telehealth has expanded access meaningfully. A number of POTS specialists now see patients virtually across state lines, which matters when the nearest autonomic clinic is several hours away.

Document everything. Request copies of every visit note and every test result. Patient portals usually provide these. Having a written record of what was said, what was tested, and what was declined creates a paper trail that future providers will take more seriously than your verbal account.

## What this article is, and isn't

This article is not a substitute for a doctor. It cannot diagnose you. The questions of whether you have POTS, what subtype you might have, what treatment approach makes sense for your specific situation — these are all clinical questions that require evaluation by someone who can examine you and review your full history.

What this article *is*, hopefully, is permission to stop wondering whether you are imagining this.

You are not imagining it. The thing that is happening to you is real and measurable. There is a name for it. The diagnostic criteria are published in major medical journals. The test takes ten minutes. The condition is treatable, and most patients see meaningful improvement once they have the right diagnosis and the right care.

You have spent years being told that what is happening to you is not serious. It is serious. You have spent years being told that the problem is in your head. It is not in your head — it is in your autonomic nervous system, which controls your heart rate when you stand up.

If you have read this far, you probably already suspect this. The next step is to find a clinician who will perform the active stand test. That clinician may not be the one you've been seeing. That is okay.
